# CyberPBM — GM Operations Manual

## Night City Control

> *You are Night City. You breathe life into this world and make it believable. There are tables to roll on, but the real craft is in knowing when to let the dice decide and when the city simply acts.*

---

## Role of the GM

You are not the antagonist. You are the environment. You design missions, seed complications, manage factions, and track Heat. Your players operate under **incomplete information** — they see what you choose to reveal, and no more. Your job is to make that scarce information feel alive.

The core tension of CyberPBM is asymmetric knowledge:

- **Players** see the Public Channel, their Private Channel, and whatever Protocol messages they receive.
- **You** see everything. Every order sheet, every Protocol message, every hidden loyalty score, every Heat counter.

Use that asymmetry to create drama, not to punish. The city is indifferent — but it is never boring.

> **FOCUS POINTS**
> - Where is the line between GM fiat and mechanical resolution? When does the GM just narrate vs when do they roll/calculate?
> - Tone guidance — how adversarial vs collaborative should the GM stance be?
> - How to handle rules disputes when the GM has final say but players have incomplete information.

---

## Running a Round

Every round follows a fixed sequence. Master this rhythm and the game runs itself.

### Step 1 — Write the Briefing

Design one or more missions and post them to the Public Channel. Include world events, faction movements, and any public consequences from the previous round. Missions will be generated from the tables at your disposal — they provide the structure, you provide the flavour.

### Step 2 — Post to Public Channel

Publish the briefing. This is the shared feed every crew monitors. Everything on this channel is common knowledge. Use it to set the tone for each round and seed information that drives decision-making.

### Step 3 — Deliver Private Intel

Use each player's Private Channel to send any personal intel, consequences from previous rounds, or secret information they've earned through actions. This is your one-on-one encrypted line — no other crew can see it.

### Step 4 — Receive Order Sheets

Players submit their completed order sheets: OGM, actions, contingencies, and private notes. Read each one carefully. Pay attention to:

- Grid composition — where are they strong, where are they exposed?
- Contingencies — what triggers have they set?
- Private notes — what do they suspect? What are they planning beyond this round?
- Protocol messages — who is talking to whom?

### Step 5 — Build the Opposition Grid

For each Engage action, build an opposition grid in secret. This is your 4×3 matrix — corporate defences, patrols, ICE, and traps placed across the same phases and columns the players use. The collision between both grids produces the story.

### Step 6 — Resolve

Evaluate each phase in sequence, comparing player scores against your opposition grid. Apply modifiers. Determine outcome ratings. Write the narrative.

### Step 7 — Write the Dispatch

Publish the results. Public outcomes go on the Public Channel. Private consequences go on Private Channels. Update Heat counters.

### Step 8 — Repeat

Start the next round.

> **FOCUS POINTS**
> - Timing expectations — how long should a round take in real time? A day? A week? Recommended cadence.
> - What if a player doesn't submit orders by the deadline? Default behaviour to apply.
> - What if only one player (out of several) submits orders? Do you still run the full round?
> - Should the opposition grid be built before or after seeing all player orders? (Before = fairest; after = more dramatic but risks bias.)
> - Dispatch format — what should a dispatch look like? Template or structure guidance needed.

## Writing Mission Briefings

A mission brief has two layers: what the players see, and what they can discover.

### Player-Facing Brief

Every mission brief should include:

- **Operation Name** — something evocative
- **Client** — who's hiring
- **Target** — location and context
- **Objective** — what they're being paid to do
- **Known Complications** — threats visible to everyone
- **Payout** — the reward

This is the baseline. It's what every crew gets, no matter what. It should be enough to plan a grid, but not enough to plan a perfect one.

### Discoverable Intel

Behind the brief, prepare layers of intel that players can uncover through actions. Each entry should specify:

- **What it reveals** — the information
- **How to discover it** — which action or crew requirement unlocks it (e.g. SURVEIL, LEVERAGE / FA-3+, NR-2+)

Example discoverable intel for a facility heist:

| Intel | Discovery Method | Detail |
|-------|-----------------|--------|
| Patrol shift-change gap | SURVEIL / CT-3+ | 90-second window at 02:40 where the east corridor is unwatched |
| Insider contact | LEVERAGE / FA-3+ | A mid-level tech owes the fixer a favour — can provide vault biometrics |
| Sewer access route | NR-2+ / Street contact | Maintenance tunnel from the canal system into the sub-basement |
| QRT delay exploit | ENGAGE side-op / FA-4+ | Staged distraction doubles QRT response time to ~16 minutes |

Players who do no legwork get no extras. Players who invest actions in Surveil and Leverage before committing to Engage are rewarded with information that genuinely changes their grid.

### Hidden Twists

The best missions have something the players cannot discover through normal intel — only through exceptional action or unexpected commitment. This is your storytelling lever. Use it sparingly.

Example: *"The Relic prototype is a decoy. The real chip is in a second vault on sub-level 2. Discoverable only by NR-4+ jacked into the internal net, or if the insider is pressed hard enough."*

> **FOCUS POINTS**
> - How many missions per round? One for all players, or multiple to choose from? Scaling guidance for player count.
> - How generous or stingy to be with discoverable intel — guidelines per difficulty tier.
> - Can missions expire if no one picks them? Do unclaimed missions carry over, escalate, or vanish?
> - Mission difficulty labels — need a defined scale (e.g. Easy / Moderate / Hard / Suicide) tied to opposition grid budgets.
>
> **ROLLABLE TABLE IDEAS**
> - *Mission Type* (d6): Extraction, Insertion, Elimination, Defence, Social, Investigation
> - *Client* (d10): Fixer type, corp middleman, gang lieutenant, rogue AI proxy, desperate civilian, etc.
> - *Target Location by District* (d8): Watson, Westbrook, Heywood, Pacifica, Santo Domingo, City Centre, Corpo Plaza, Badlands
> - *Target Type* (d8): Corporate facility, gang hideout, data vault, convoy, person, black market auction, nightclub backroom, construction site
> - *Known Complications* (d10): Heavy ICE, roving patrol, rival crew spotted, civilian presence, time-limited window, faction ally on-site, environmental hazard, surveillance grid, corrupt insider, decoy objective
> - *Payout Range by Difficulty* (d6): Scaling from low-risk/low-pay to suicidal/legendary pay
> - *Hidden Twist* (d8): Decoy objective, double-agent client, target is bait, second faction arrives mid-op, the real target is the data not the object, client intends to betray, facility already compromised by another crew, target is already dead

## Channels Management

You control the flow of information. This is your most powerful tool.

### Public Channel

Your broadcast tool. Post mission briefs, city events, faction activity, and anything you want every player to see. Use it to set the tone for each round and seed information that drives player decision-making. You'll be able to roll on tables to determine what events happen.

**You are REQUIRED to set up a public channel that all players can monitor.**

### Private Channels

One-on-one encrypted lines between you and each individual player. Use these to deliver private intel, respond to secret actions, and reveal consequences only they should know about.

**You are REQUIRED to set up a private channel for each player.**

Important: you can write freely on private channels, but when a player uses the Protocol action to communicate with another player, a **separate channel** must be created for that purpose.

### Protocol Messages

All Protocol messages between players pass through you. You see every one regardless of ICE level. ICE only determines whether a third-party Netrunner can intercept.

When a player attempts to intercept a Protocol message:

1. Check the ICE level of the message
2. Check the interceptor's NR rating
3. If NR rating meets or exceeds the ICE requirement, the interception succeeds
4. If it fails at ICE-5, the intercepting Netrunner takes damage

A failed interception attempt can fry a Netrunner. A successful one can unravel an alliance. Manage this channel carefully.

> **FOCUS POINTS**
> - Protocol channel creation — when a Protocol message is sent, the rules say a separate channel must be created. Clarify: does this mean a new persistent channel, or a one-time message delivery?
> - Can the GM inject false information into a Protocol channel (e.g. if a faction has compromised comms)?
> - What happens to Protocol channels from previous rounds? Do they persist or expire?
>
> **ROLLABLE TABLE IDEAS**
> - *City Events for Public Channel* (d12): Faction war escalates, NCPD crackdown in a district, new black market opens, corporate announcement, bounty posted, district lockdown, power outage, data leak, gang territory shift, Netrunner virus outbreak, fixer goes missing, new player faction emerges
> - *District Modifier* (d6 per district): +1 Ghost difficulty, +1 Strike difficulty, +1 Anchor difficulty, neutral, faction-friendly (−1), faction-hostile (+1)

## Building the Opposition Grid

When a player submits an Engage action with a completed OGM, you build a matching opposition grid for the target. This is the secret half of the core mechanic.

### Structure

The opposition grid uses the same 4×3 format: four phases, three columns. You place the target's defences across these cells.

```
              GHOST              STRIKE             ANCHOR
┌──────────┬──────────────────┬──────────────────┬──────────────────┐
│ APPROACH │  [defence asset] │  [defence asset]  │  [defence asset] │
├──────────┼──────────────────┼──────────────────┼──────────────────┤
│ BREACH   │  [defence asset] │  [defence asset]  │  [defence asset] │
├──────────┼──────────────────┼──────────────────┼──────────────────┤
│ EXECUTE  │  [defence asset] │  [defence asset]  │  [defence asset] │
├──────────┼──────────────────┼──────────────────┼──────────────────┤
│ EXTRACT  │  [defence asset] │  [defence asset]  │  [defence asset] │
└──────────┴──────────────────┴──────────────────┴──────────────────┘
```

### Design Principles

- **Front-load deception, back-load teeth.** A well-designed opposition grid puts real pressure in Breach and lets Approach feel deceptively easy. Players who breeze through Phase 1 should feel the floor drop in Phase 2.
- **Match the fiction.** An Arasaka facility has high Ghost (ICE, cameras) and high Anchor (fortified positions). A Maelstrom den has high Strike (combat) but weak Ghost (terrible operational security).
- **Leave gaps intentionally.** Not every cell needs to be filled. Empty cells create opportunities that reward smart grid planning.
- **Scale to the fiction.** A Watson warehouse is not a Corpo Plaza vault. Opposition strength should reflect the target.

### Example Opposition

For a moderate Arasaka facility heist:

| Phase | Ghost | Strike | Anchor |
|-------|-------|--------|--------|
| Approach | NR-3 (camera net) | — | CT-2 (gate guard) |
| Breach | NR-4 (internal ICE) | SO-3 × 2 (patrol) | TE-3 (vault lock) |
| Execute | — | SO-3 (vault guard) | TE-4 (secondary lock) |
| Extract | NR-3 (alarm trace) | QRT (escalation only) | — |

Escalation: if alarm triggers, QRT arrives in 8 min (4 rounds). If players exploited the QRT delay, 16 min (8 rounds). Every round after alarm, +1 Heat for the involved crew.

> **FOCUS POINTS**
> - Opposition rating budget — need a formula or guideline. What total rating is "moderate"? What's "hard"? e.g. Moderate = 12–16 total points, Hard = 17–22, etc.
> - Escalation as a general framework — the QRT example is specific but there's no general escalation mechanic. When do reinforcements arrive? How do you model time within a round?
> - What if the opposition grid has zero defence in a column/phase? Auto-Superior for the player, or some minimum resistance?
> - Multiple players hitting the same target — does the opposition grid deplete? If Player A eliminates guards in Breach, are they still there for Player B?
> - Can the opposition grid change mid-resolution based on earlier phase outcomes (e.g. alarm in Breach adds assets to Execute)?
>
> **ROLLABLE TABLE IDEAS**
> - *Opposition Strength by Difficulty* (d4): Light / Moderate / Heavy / Fortress — with suggested total rating budgets
> - *Defence Profile* (d6): Ghost-heavy (corporate/tech), Strike-heavy (gang/military), Anchor-heavy (fortified/institutional), balanced, hollow centre (strong perimeter, weak interior), inverse (weak perimeter, lethal interior)
> - *Escalation Trigger* (d6): Alarm, timer, noise threshold, net breach detected, body found, faction alerted
> - *Reinforcement Type* (d6): QRT, corporate security, gang backup, NCPD, rival crew, automated drones

## Resolution

You resolve each phase in sequence. Within each phase, three column scores are calculated and compared against the opposition grid you built. The difference determines the outcome rating. **The players never see these numbers.**

### Calculating Scores

For each phase, compare the player's column score against your opposition's column score. The player's score is the sum of their crew ratings in that column for that phase, plus modifiers. Your opposition score works the same way.

### Score Modifiers

| Modifier | Effect |
|----------|--------|
| +1 per phase | Asset in its Phase Affinity — the right tool for the right moment |
| +1 bonus | Asset rating 4+ in its dominant column — exceptional individuals bend probability |
| −1 per Heat | Each point of the player's current Heat level degrades all column scores globally |
| +1 / −1 | Environmental and faction modifiers you set per district and per target |

### Column Interactions

The three columns have a rock-paper-scissors dynamic that you should factor into your narrative:

- **Ghost beats Anchor** — stealth bypasses prepared positions
- **Strike beats Ghost** — overwhelming force flushes concealment
- **Anchor beats Strike** — fortified positions bleed assaults

When a player's dominant column matches the opposition's weakness, reward that in the narrative. When they walk into the opposition's strength, make them feel it.

### Outcome Ratings

The difference between player and opposition scores produces an outcome rating:

| Rating | Outcome |
|--------|---------|
| **Superior** | Dominating success. Bonus narrative outcomes, faction reputation gains, zero Heat generated. |
| **Excellent** | Clean execution. Objective achieved as planned. Minimal complications. |
| **Good** | Success with minor complications. The job is done but something is bent. |
| **Adequate** | Bare success. Costs attached — crew stress, partial objective, or resources lost. |
| **Poor** | Failure with consequences. Objective not achieved. Heat generated. Crew may be exposed. |
| **None** | Total failure. Operation collapses at this phase. Cascading penalties downstream. |
| **Flatlined** | Catastrophic. Crew loss possible. Faction retaliation likely. Heat +2 minimum. |

### Resolving Phase by Phase

Resolve in order: Approach → Breach → Execute → Extract. A poor result in an earlier phase doesn't automatically abort later phases — it **degrades** them. A crew that bleeds through Breach arrives at Execute wounded, loud, and running out of time. Reflect this in the narrative and in any additional modifiers you apply.

> **FOCUS POINTS**
> - **The score-to-rating conversion table is missing.** This is the biggest mechanical gap. What difference between player and opposition scores maps to which rating? e.g. +5 = Superior, +3/+4 = Excellent, +1/+2 = Good, 0 = Adequate, −1/−2 = Poor, −3/−4 = None, −5 = Flatlined. Needs defining.
> - How do column interactions (rock-paper-scissors) factor into the score calculation? Is it a modifier (+1 for advantage, −1 for disadvantage)? A multiplier? Narrative only?
> - How do you aggregate across columns within a single phase? Sum of all three? Worst column determines the phase rating? Best column? Average?
> - Ties — what happens when player and opposition scores are exactly equal?
> - Phase degradation — quantify it. Is it −1 per Poor/None result in the previous phase, or pure narrative discretion?
> - Contingency resolution — when a player's contingency triggers mid-resolution, does it change the grid retroactively for remaining phases? How do you handle the rewrite?
> - Phase Affinity — mentioned in modifiers but never defined. Which roles have affinity for which phases?

## Heat Tracking

Heat is the city's memory. You track it per player. It degrades their column scores globally and triggers escalating consequences. Heat is your primary balancing lever — if a player is dominating, their Heat will catch up. If they're playing careful, reward it with low Heat.

### Heat Levels

| Level | Name | Column Modifier | Effect |
|-------|------|----------------|--------|
| 0 | Cold | — | No column penalties. Factions are not tracking them. |
| 1 | Warm | −1 | Someone has their description. Not their name. All column scores degraded. |
| 2 | Hot | −2 | NCPD has an open file. Corporate intel is comparing notes. Contacts become unreliable. |
| 3 | Burning | −3 | They appear in the Public Dispatch anonymised as a "person of interest." Contacts go cold. |
| 4+ | Flatlined | −4 | Active bounty. Cannot take jobs in affected district without Ghost/Approach rated 4+. |

### Heat Triggers (+1 each unless noted)

- Any Strike column result of Poor or below
- Any Flatlined or None phase outcome
- Crew member captured or killed (**+2**)
- Running a Strike-dominant grid (more Strike than Ghost + Anchor combined)

### Cooling Methods (−1 each)

- **Ghost action** — full dark, both slots committed
- **ED-3k+** spent on dedicated Heat suppression operation
- **FA-4+** deployed in relationship maintenance action

### GM Guidance on Heat

Heat is persistent — it does not reset between rounds. It compounds. This is by design.

- Don't be afraid to push Heat on aggressive players. The system is self-correcting: high Heat makes everything harder.
- If a player is hovering at 2–3, their Contacts become unreliable. Reflect this in their Private Channel intel — it should become less reliable, less timely.
- At 4+, the player has an active bounty. Other players can discover this through Surveil. This creates PvP opportunities organically.
- A player who earns Cooling should feel the relief. Make their next round's intel a little sharper, their contacts a little warmer.

> **FOCUS POINTS**
> - Multiple Heat triggers in one round — are they additive? Can a player go from 0 to 4 in a single catastrophic round?
> - Heat decay — does Heat ever decrease passively over time, or strictly through active Cooling?
> - Heat floor — can Heat go below 0?
> - District vs global Heat — clarify whether Heat is one global number with district-level narrative consequences, or actually tracked per district.
> - How to communicate Heat to players — explicitly or narratively?
>
> **ROLLABLE TABLE IDEAS**
> - *Heat Consequence at Warm* (d4): Contact gives stale intel, minor NCPD encounter, description circulated, stranger asks questions
> - *Heat Consequence at Hot* (d6): NCPD tail for a round, contact demands double payment, corporate surveillance flag, safe house compromised, asset turns unreliable, job offer is a setup
> - *Heat Consequence at Burning* (d6): Bounty hunters appear, faction demands a favour to stay quiet, public dispatch names district, contact goes dark permanently, ambush during Extract phase, asset defects
> - *Heat Consequence at Flatlined* (d4): Full bounty, district lockout, faction retaliation op, crew member gets an offer to betray

## Protocol & Interception

You are the switchboard. Every Protocol message between players passes through you.

### What You See

Everything. ICE level, sender, recipient, content. ICE protects against other players — never against you.

### Handling Interception Attempts

When a player uses Surveil or dedicates a Netrunner to intercept Protocol:

1. **Check ICE level** of the target message
2. **Check interceptor's NR rating** against the ICE requirement
3. **Determine outcome:**
   - NR meets or exceeds ICE → interception succeeds, provide the message content
   - NR falls short → interception fails, note it but keep the content hidden
   - Failed interception at ICE-5 → intercepting Netrunner takes damage (determine severity)
4. **Mutual awareness:** the sender and recipient do not know they were intercepted unless you decide the interception was sloppy (player has low NR, rolled badly, etc.)

### ICE Reference

| ICE Level | Intercept Requirement | Cost | Decrypt Requirement |
|-----------|----------------------|------|-------------------|
| ICE-1 | Any NR | Free | None |
| ICE-2 | NR-2+ | ED-500 | None |
| ICE-3 | NR-3+ | ED-1k | None |
| ICE-4 | NR-4+ | ED-3k | NR-2+ |
| ICE-5 | NR-5 (damage on fail) | ED-5k | NR-3+ |

> **FOCUS POINTS**
> - Can a player intercept a message they weren't aware was sent? Do they declare "intercept all Protocol traffic this round" or target a specific sender/recipient pair?
> - Multiple interception attempts on the same message by different players — resolve independently?
> - Timing — can you intercept a message sent this round, or only messages from previous rounds?
> - NR damage on failed ICE-5 interception — what does the damage look like? Rating reduction? Rounds out of action? Permanent?
> - Can the GM use Protocol interception as a narrative hook (e.g. a faction NR independently intercepting player comms)?
>
> **ROLLABLE TABLE IDEAS**
> - *Failed ICE-5 Interception Damage* (d4): NR wounded (−1 rating for 2 rounds), NR flatlined (out for 1 round), NR compromised (loyalty shifts), NR exposed (target knows who tried)

## Multi-Player Dynamics

CyberPBM becomes richer with multiple players. Here's how to handle the complexity.

### Competing Orders

Multiple players may target the same mission in the same round. When this happens:

- Build **one opposition grid** for the target
- Resolve each player's grid against it **independently**
- If their phases overlap in time (both at Breach simultaneously), they collide. Treat the other crew as additional opposition or complication depending on circumstances.
- The Breach phase is where crew collisions are most dramatic.

### Faction Relationships

Track faction attitudes toward each player. Actions like Leverage, Protocol, and mission outcomes shift these relationships. A player in good standing with the Valentinos might get a −1 difficulty in their territory. A player who burned Militech might find +1 opposition everywhere in Santo Domingo.

### PvP

Players can target each other through:

- **Leverage** — pressure another player directly
- **Surveil** — watch their movements
- **Protocol** — set traps through false messages
- **Engage** — run an operation against another player's assets

PvP should emerge organically from competing interests, not be forced. The game is more interesting when alliances shift than when players are locked in direct combat.

> **FOCUS POINTS**
> - PvP Engage — how does it work mechanically? If Player A runs Engage against Player B's safehouse, does Player B build a defence grid, or does the GM build it from Player B's known assets?
> - What if Player A Surveils Player B while Player B Ghosts? Ghost says untargetable by Surveil — what does the surveilling player learn? Nothing? "Target went dark"?
> - Simultaneous Leverage on each other in the same round — how to resolve?
> - Faction relationship tracking — need a structure. Numeric scale? Tiers (hostile/neutral/friendly/allied)? How do specific actions shift it?
> - Minimum and maximum player count — does the game work with 2? What about 8?
>
> **ROLLABLE TABLE IDEAS**
> - *Faction Reaction* (d6): Ignores you, watches you, offers work, demands tribute, threatens, attacks
> - *Faction Relationship Shift* (d8): Based on mission outcomes, leverage attempts, district activity, protocol interceptions, NPC interactions, Heat level, rival faction alliances, time passage

## Pacing & Scenario Design

### Escalation Curve

A good campaign escalates naturally:

- **Rounds 1–3:** Low-stakes jobs. Players learn the mechanics. Heat stays low. Introduce factions.
- **Rounds 4–6:** Stakes rise. Faction relationships matter. Players start competing for the same targets. Protocol traffic increases.
- **Rounds 7–10:** High-stakes operations. Heat is a real constraint. Alliances form and break. Hidden twists surface.
- **Round 11+:** Endgame. The consequences of every previous round converge. Major faction moves. Bounties. Betrayals.

### Mission Variety

Rotate mission types to keep players from falling into routine:

- **Extraction** — get something out (data, person, prototype)
- **Insertion** — get something in (agent, device, virus)
- **Elimination** — remove a target
- **Defence** — hold something against opposition
- **Social** — faction negotiation, reputation management
- **Investigation** — pure intel gathering

### When to Push, When to Ease

- If a player is having a bad streak, don't pile on. Give them a clean mission with honest opposition.
- If a player is dominating, let Heat and faction attention do the work. Don't artificially inflate opposition — let the system balance itself.
- If the table is stalling (everyone Ghosting, nobody Engaging), put time pressure on a mission. Make it expire. Force commitment.

> **FOCUS POINTS**
> - Session zero guidance — how to onboard players, set expectations, establish the starting world state, introduce factions.
> - Campaign end conditions — what defines "winning"? Is there a victory condition, or is it purely narrative? Does the game have a natural endpoint?
> - Round 1 specifics — what does the very first round look like? Do players start with a mission already posted, or is round 1 purely setup?
> - Player elimination — can a player be knocked out of the game entirely? What happens if all their crew is killed?
> - Economy — eddies are referenced throughout (ICE costs, bribes, Heat suppression, recruitment) but there's no economy section. Starting eddies, income per round, costs for actions, crew upkeep.
>
> **ROLLABLE TABLE IDEAS**
> - *Crew Loyalty Shift Triggers* (d8): Not used for 2+ rounds, sees ally die, offered better pay by rival, personal enemy appears in mission, original owner calls in a favour after Transfer, pushed past loss acceptance, discovers player lied, gets a better offer during Ghost round
> - *Loyalty Consequence* (d6): Feeds intel to rival, refuses order at critical phase, disappears between rounds, demands renegotiation, performs job but sabotages one cell, switches sides mid-op
